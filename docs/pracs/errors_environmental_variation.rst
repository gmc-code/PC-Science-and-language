============================
Environmental Variation
============================

| **Environmental variation** occurs when uncontrolled conditions in the
| experimental setting introduce unpredictable changes into measurements
| between trials.
| It is most commonly classified as a **random error** — fluctuations in
| the environment vary unpredictably in size and direction between readings.
| It can become **systematic** if an environmental condition is consistently
| different from the intended condition across all trials.

Examples:

* Room temperature gradually rising during a long experiment, causing
  readings taken later in the session to differ from those taken earlier
* Air currents near a top-pan balance causing the display to fluctuate
  between readings
* Variation in ambient light levels affecting the readings of a
  light-dependent resistor or colorimeter between trials
* Humidity changes between sessions causing the mass of a hygroscopic
  substance to vary slightly between repeated measurements
* Electrical noise from nearby equipment introducing fluctuations into
  the readings of a sensitive voltage or current sensor
* Vibrations from foot traffic or nearby machinery causing instability
  in balance readings between trials

.. admonition:: Environmental Variation: A Four-Step Analysis
    :class: premise

    Use the four-step framework to analyse environmental variation:

    **Step 1 — Identify the source**
        *Environment* — uncontrolled conditions in the experimental
        setting introduce variation that is independent of the instrument,
        the operator, or the method design. Name the specific
        environmental variable rather than writing "environmental error"
        — for example, "room temperature fluctuation" or "air currents
        near the balance."

    **Step 2 — Classify the behaviour**
        In most cases: unpredictable spread → **Random error** → affects
        precision. If the environmental condition is consistently
        different from the intended condition across all trials, it
        becomes a consistent, one-direction shift → **Systematic error**
        → affects accuracy.

    **Step 3 — Explain the impact**
        When random: results are scattered above and below the true value.
        No single trial is consistently too high or too low — the
        variation averages out over many measurements, but precision is
        reduced.

        When systematic: all results are shifted in the same direction
        — for example, if the room is consistently warmer than the
        specified temperature, all readings involving temperature-sensitive
        quantities will be consistently displaced.

    **Step 4 — Suggest an improvement**
        Random environmental variation is reduced by controlling or
        monitoring environmental conditions and increasing the number of
        trials. Systematic environmental bias is eliminated by identifying
        and correcting the condition before data collection.

----

Effect
------

Environmental variation most commonly produces **random scatter** around
the true value. Because environmental fluctuations vary unpredictably
between trials:

* results are scattered above and below the true value rather than
  consistently displaced in one direction,
* **precision is reduced** — repeated readings may not agree closely
  with each other,
* **accuracy is not affected** when the error is random — there is no
  consistent bias; results are centred on the true value on average,
* repeating measurements and averaging will reduce the effect, since
  random fluctuations tend to cancel out over many trials,
* if the environmental condition is consistently offset from the
  intended value, the error becomes systematic and **accuracy is also
  reduced** — repeating measurements will not help in this case.

----

Improvements
------------

To reduce environmental variation, control or monitor environmental
conditions throughout data collection.

* Conduct the experiment in a temperature-controlled environment, or
  record ambient temperature at regular intervals and account for
  drift in the analysis.
* Shield sensitive instruments from air currents using a draught
  enclosure or by closing windows and vents near the apparatus.
* Allow instruments and samples to equilibrate to room temperature
  before taking measurements, particularly for mass and temperature
  readings.
* Use a Faraday cage or shielded cables to reduce electromagnetic
  interference affecting sensitive electrical measurements.
* Complete all trials in a single session where possible to minimise
  changes in environmental conditions between measurements.
* Record environmental conditions (temperature, humidity, light level)
  alongside data so variation can be identified and discussed in the
  evaluation.
