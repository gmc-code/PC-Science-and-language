===========================
Cause and Effect Patterns
===========================

Writers choose different cause-and-effect structures depending on the purpose, audience, and level of formality of their text.
The table below shows which forms are most appropriate in each context, along with examples and explanations of how these choices support clarity and communication.

.. list-table::
    :widths: 20 25 35 40
    :header-rows: 1

    * - Context
      - Forms
      - Cause-Effect Structures
      - Features and Benefits

    * - Lab reports
      - Implied connection, verb forms
      -
        - A. B.
        - nomA causes nomB
        - nomA results in nomB
      - Objective, concise

    * - Scientific explanations
      - Verb forms (“causes”, “results in”)
      -
        - nomA causes nomB
        - nomA results in nomB
      - Precise, mechanism-focused

    * - News articles
      - Text connectives
      -
        - A as a result B
        - A consequently B
        - A therefore B
      - Clear flow for general readers

    * - Everyday speech (cause → effect)
      - Conjunctions (“so”, “hence”)
      -
        - A so B
        - A hence B
      - Natural, conversational

    * - Everyday speech (effect → cause)
      - Conjunctions (“because”, “since”)
      -
        - B because A
        - B since A
      - Natural, conversational; emphasises outcomes

    * - Academic essays
      - Prepositional phrases, noun phrases
      -
        - because A, B
        - due to nomA, B
        - as a result of nomA, B
        - the result of nomA is nomB
        - the cause of nomB is nomA
        - the reason for nomB is nomA
        - the cause of nomB is nomA
      - Formal, structured

    * - Historical or analytical writing
      - “results from”, “as a consequence of”
      -
        - nomB results from nomA
        - nomB occurs as a consequence of nomA
      - Emphasises outcomes
