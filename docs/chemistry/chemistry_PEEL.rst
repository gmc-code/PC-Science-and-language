Chemistry -- PEEL and SFG Paragraph Structures
================================================

Peel and SFG Paragraph Structures for Writing Information Reports in Chemistry are explained in this section. The examples focus on physical and chemical changes, but the structures can be applied to any topic  of science more broadly.

PEEL Paragraph Structure
---------------------------

| PEEL is a paragraph structure used to help students write clear, logical, well-developed paragraphs.
| Each letter stands for a different part of the paragraph:

P - Point
    | The first sentence makes the main point of the paragraph.
    | It introduces the topic or idea that will be explained in the rest of the paragraph.
    | It tells the reader exactly what the paragraph will be about.

E - Explanation
    | The next sentence explains the point in more detail.
    | It answers: How does it work? Why does it happen? What does it mean?

E - Evidence / Example
    | This sentence gives a concrete example, observation, or piece of evidence.
    | It shows the idea in action.

L - Link
    | The final sentence links back to the main idea or topic.
    | It wraps up the paragraph and reinforces the key message.

SFG Paragraph Structure
---------------------------

In SFG, this pattern is staged structure of an explanatory paragraph:
    | HyperTheme (Topic sentence / Point)
    | Explanation (How the process works)
    | Elaboration (Clarifying detail)
    | Generalisation (Link)

Information Report: Types of Physical Changes
------------------------------------------------

| Read the following information report about physical changes.
| This information report uses a PEEL-style paragraph structure, which aligns closely with the SFG stages of HyperTheme, Explanation, Elaboration, and Generalisation.
| Each paragraph begins with a clear topic sentence, explains the scientific idea, provides an example, and finishes by linking back to the main concept of physical change.


Physical changes are changes that alter the form or appearance of a substance without creating anything new. These changes can be grouped into several categories; three common examples are changes in state, size, or shape.


Changes of state occur when a substance moves between solid, liquid, and gas. These changes happen because particles gain or lose energy, causing them to move closer together or further apart. For example, when ice melts into water, the particles gain energy and move more freely, even though they remain the same type of particles. This shows that state changes modify particle arrangement and movement without changing the substance itself.

Changes in size happen when a material is cut, broken, shredded, or divided into smaller pieces. These actions rearrange the particles but do not alter what they are made of. For instance, when a piece of chalk is snapped in half, both pieces are still made of the same material even though they are smaller. This demonstrates that size changes do not create new substances, because the particles remain unchanged.

Changes in shape involve bending, folding, stretching, or inflating a material. These changes affect how the particles are arranged but do not change the particles themselves. For example, when a balloon is inflated, the rubber stretches into a new shape, yet the material remains rubber. This confirms that shape changes alter appearance only, not the substance itself.

PEEL Paragraphs on Types of Physical Changes
-----------------------------------------------

Introduction
------------
**Point:**
Physical changes are changes that alter the form or appearance of a substance without creating anything new.

**Explanation:**
In all physical changes, the particles remain the same even if their arrangement shifts, meaning the substance keeps its identity.

**Example:**
These changes can be grouped into several categories, and three common examples are changes in state, size, and shape.

**Link:**
These categories show that physical changes can look different on the surface, but they all involve the same particles being rearranged rather than transformed into something new.


Changes of State
----------------
**Point:**
Changes of state occur when a substance moves between solid, liquid, and gas.

**Explanation:**
These changes happen because particles gain or lose energy, causing them to move closer together or further apart.

**Example:**
For example, when ice melts into water, the particles gain energy and move more freely, even though they remain the same type of particles.

**Link:**

Text and Paragraph openers
============================

1. The Text Opener (General Statement)
--------------------------------------

The text opener is the first sentence or first short paragraph. Its job is to tell the reader what the whole report is about. It introduces the topic in a clear, general way. It uses a topic sentence that comes first in the paragraph.

Example pattern:

   Physical changes are changes that alter a substance without forming something new.

This opener gives the reader the big idea before any details appear.

2. Paragraph Openers
-----------------------

Each paragraph begins with a hyperTheme — a sentence that tells the reader what that paragraph will focus on.

A good paragraph opener:

- Names the subtopic (e.g., state changes, size changes, shape changes)
- Signals what kind of information will follow
- Keeps the text cohesive by linking back to the main topic

Example patterns:

   | Changes of state occur when a substance moves between solid, liquid, and gas.
   | Changes in size happen when a material is cut or broken.
   | Changes in shape involve bending, folding, or stretching a material.

These openers keep the reader oriented, maintain a clear Theme pattern, and build the report in a logical, predictable way.

3. Why This Matters
-------------------

| The text opener sets the overall Theme of the whole report.
| The paragraph openers set the Theme for each section.
| This structure helps the reader understand the main ideas and how they connect. It makes the report easier to follow and more engaging to read.

Information Report: Four Signs of a Chemical Reaction
=====================================================

Chemical reactions are processes where new substances are formed from existing materials. These reactions can be recognised through several observable indicators that show the particles have rearranged into something new. Four common signs of a chemical reaction are permanent colour change, gas being given off, formation of a precipitate, and temperature change. Each indicator provides evidence that a chemical change has occurred.

Permanent colour change is one clear sign that a chemical reaction has taken place. This type of colour change does not reverse when the conditions return to normal, because a new substance has formed. For example, when iron rusts, the grey metal surface turns reddish-brown as iron reacts with oxygen to form iron oxide. The new colour shows that a new substance has been produced.

Gas being given off is another important indicator of chemical change. Bubbles, fizzing, or a noticeable gas release suggest that the reactants have formed a new substance in gas form. A simple example is the reaction between vinegar and bicarbonate of soda, which produces carbon dioxide gas. The rapid bubbling shows that a chemical reaction is occurring.

Formation of a precipitate also signals that a chemical reaction has happened. A precipitate is a solid that appears when two clear solutions react and form an insoluble substance. For instance, when solutions of silver nitrate and sodium chloride are mixed, a white solid called silver chloride forms and settles out. The sudden appearance of this solid indicates the creation of a new substance.

Temperature change is a further sign of chemical reaction, as energy is either released or absorbed when new substances form. The reaction may feel warmer (exothermic) or cooler (endothermic) depending on the energy flow. A common example is the reaction inside instant cold packs, where ammonium nitrate dissolves and absorbs heat, causing the pack to become cold. The temperature shift shows that a chemical change has taken place.
